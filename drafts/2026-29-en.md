## THE INSIGHT

Your hiring process keeps getting longer. More rounds. More assessments. Now a three-day work trial, unpaid, before anyone signs an offer. Each added round reveals less about whether the candidate can actually do the job.

The conventional reading is that AI has flooded applications and made them unreliable. [Fast Company](https://www.fastcompany.com/91530997/the-rise-of-days-long-and-often-unpaid-work-trials-for-job-applicants) quotes a training platform CEO describing this as the largest upheaval in modern hiring history. That part is real. It is also incomplete.

The deeper problem is that your company cannot describe its own work. When the work lives only inside the practice of it, the only way to evaluate a candidate is to watch them practice. Every extra round of interviewing is a workaround for a missing artifact. You are using a candidate's unpaid time to do the documentation your operating model never produced.

Most advisors call this a hiring problem and prescribe better assessments. It is not a hiring problem. It is a missing document.

**Job Description vs. Work Description**

A job description names the role: responsibilities, requirements, reporting lines. A work description names the work itself: the inputs that arrive, the decisions that get made, the outputs that leave. Most companies have the first. Very few have the second.

The work description is an Operating Model OS artifact. When it exists, three things change. Hiring gets shorter, because you can evaluate a candidate's judgment against a written reference instead of three days of observation. Onboarding gets faster, because a new hire reads the work description and knows what good looks like in week one. Departures get survivable, because the work description is what the person leaves behind when they go.

The test is narrow. Could a qualified outsider read one document and understand not just what the role does, but what a good decision inside the role looks like? If not, you have a job posting. You do not have a work description.

Most owners refuse to write this because they believe the work is too nuanced to put on paper. That belief is the Operating Model OS problem in one sentence. The nuance is real. The refusal to encode it is what keeps you trapped in founder-dependent hiring and multi-day evaluations.

**This week**

Pick one role on your team that feels hardest to hire for. Sit with the person doing it now. Ask three questions. What decisions do you make in a normal week? Which ones are hard? What would a new person get wrong in month one? Write the answers on a single page. That page is the first draft of your work description. It is also the document that will end most of your three-day interviews.

## THE FIELD REPORT

The [National Center for the Middle Market](https://middlemarketgrowth.org/podcast-national-center-middle-market-four-decision-makers/) published research this week identifying four distinct decision-making styles among strategic leaders in mid-market companies. Each style, according to NCMM executive director Doug Farren, links to different performance and growth outcomes.

The point is not which style is correct. The point is that a CEO's decision-making style operates as an invisible input to the operating model. An owner who decides by consensus produces a different company than an owner who decides alone and informs. Neither style is wrong. Both get encoded in the business, usually without anyone noticing.

For a US-based owner running a $5M to $100M business, the implication is practical. Your decision style is already installed in your company's operating model. The question is whether you installed it deliberately or by default. The next generation of managers needs to know the rules well enough to operate without you in the room.
