Option A - provocation

The market is starting to pay for what is encoded. Not what is performed. Not what is described in a deck. What is written down, runs without you, and survives a key person leaving on a Friday. I have sat in three diligence conversations this month where the multiple compressed at the same question. Not "what is your EBITDA." The question was simpler. Show me the document where this decision gets made. Two of the three owners did not have one. The discount started there.

THE INSIGHT

Your business looks the same to you whether it is encoded or not. Growth hides the gap. The team performs. The numbers print. You are still the one answering most of the real questions, but everyone is moving, so it feels like a system.

The buyer sees something different. The buyer is not paying for what runs. The buyer is paying for what runs without you.

The instinct is to read midmarket M&A as a multiples problem. Forvis Mazars and GF Data called 2025 "mixed signals" for a third year running. The phrase hides the real story. The 2025 numbers are the average of two distributions, not one. Businesses with documented operating models traded at one multiple. Businesses where the operating model lived in the owner's head traded at another. The spread is widening.

Most advisors call this an information problem and prescribe better data rooms. It is not an information problem. It is an encoding problem. A data room shows the buyer what you have. The operating model shows the buyer what runs. Those are different documents, and only one of them sets the price.

The Encoded Spread

What I have started calling the Encoded Spread is the gap between top-quartile and bottom-quartile multiples inside your sector. The variable that explains it is not size, growth rate, or industry. It is whether the decisions, rules, and knowledge that make the business work are written down somewhere a non-owner can read.

Three layers sit underneath the spread. The first is decision rights: who decides what, in what range, with what threshold. The second is process portability, or whether a competent person can run a typical week without you in the room. The third is knowledge persistence, or whether customer history, vendor logic, and pricing rationale live in systems or in people. Each layer compounds. A business missing all three trades at the discount. A business with all three trades at the premium.

The discomfort here is that the spread is paid for by documents you have not written, not by performance you have not delivered. You can run the company well and still trade poorly. The buyer is not pricing the year you had. The buyer is pricing the year that runs after you leave.

This week

Pick one operational area where you are the only person who can answer questions. Pricing exceptions. Vendor selection. Customer credit. Senior hiring. Write down the rule you have been using, in three sentences. Hand it to one person on your team. Tell them to make the next decision in that area without consulting you. The gap between the rule you wrote and the decision they make is what I call your operating model debt, named precisely for the first time.
