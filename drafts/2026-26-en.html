Option A - provocation

Every cofounder of xAI except the founder has walked out the door. The February reorg is already a fiction. I read the Fast Company piece twice because it names something I watch in much smaller companies every quarter. The owner confuses the chart for the machine. The chart has the roles. The chart has the lines. The chart has the names. But when the people leave, the decisions they were making leave with them, because the decisions were never in the system. They were in the person. What xAI is discovering in public is what my clients discover in private.

THE INSIGHT

You promoted her two years ago. You wrote the job description. You drew the reporting lines. She has been running the function ever since, and running it well. Last week she told you she is leaving in sixty days. Now you are staring at the role and realizing you do not know what she actually does.

This is the moment most owners discover that they built a staffing plan, not an operating model.

The two look identical from the outside. Both have roles, reporting structures, and people in seats. The difference shows up only when a seat empties. A staffing plan loses the function the moment the person walks out. An operating model keeps running because the decisions were never in the person to begin with. They were in the system.

What lives in your best manager is not just knowledge. It is a library of judgments. Which vendor to call first when the order is late. How much discretion to give a customer who asks for net-sixty. When to escalate a hire and when to move without you. She has made these calls a thousand times. They live nowhere except in her head. The company you think you are running and the company that will exist the day she leaves are not the same company.

Most advisors call this a documentation problem and hand you a template. It is not a documentation problem. It is a decision-encoding problem.

What I call the Encoded Decisions Test

For every critical role, three questions. What decisions does this role make each week that the business depends on? Where are those decisions written as rules, not as examples? Could a peer of similar seniority make the same decisions within ninety days of stepping in?

If the answer to the third question is no, you have staffing where you think you have a system. You are one resignation away from a capability gap no hire can fix in a quarter.

The rule is the artifact. Not the playbook. Not the onboarding deck. The rule: when X happens, we do Y unless Z. It is the smallest unit of an operating model, and it is what survives turnover. A company whose rules are written can lose a person and keep its judgment. A company whose rules live only in people loses both.

This week

Pick the one role you would most fear losing tomorrow. Sit with that person for an hour. Ask her to list the ten decisions she makes most often and the rule she uses for each one. Do not ask her to write a procedure. Ask her to write a rule. The gap between the rules she articulates and the decisions she actually makes is your real exposure, measured precisely.

A company that loses its senior architects mid-reorganization is not losing headcount. It is losing the decisions those people were carrying, uploaded nowhere.

For a US-based owner running a $5M to $100M business, the implication is direct. Your exposure is not the headline departure. It is the quiet one. The manager who leaves with three years of unwritten judgment is the person who takes your operating model with her.
