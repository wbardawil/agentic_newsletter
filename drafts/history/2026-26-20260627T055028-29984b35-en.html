THE APERTURA

The CEOs who thrive from here will not be the ones who adopted AI most aggressively. That is the argument Chief Executive made this week, and it cuts against almost every board conversation happening in mid-market firms right now Source. The winners, the piece argues, will be the ones who built organizations that develop judgment, exercise it at speed, and embed it into the systems making decisions around them. Notice what that sentence does not say. It does not say deployed AI. It says built organizations. The work is structural, not technical.

THE INSIGHT

You approved the AI budget. The tools were procured. The team is using them. Six months in, the margin has not moved. The decisions still land on your desk. The handoffs still break in the same places. The reports still get rewritten by hand before they reach you.

In most mid-market firms, owners read this as an adoption problem. More training. Better prompts. A different vendor. None of that fixes it, because none of that is the problem.

The conventional fix treats AI as an IT initiative. The CTO owns the rollout. The functions get access. Productivity is measured by usage. The leadership team treats the work as deployment, not redesign. That framing is the failure. It is the most expensive mistake currently moving through mid-market boardrooms.

Here is the mechanism. Your operating model was built around a human bottleneck. You, or someone close to you, processed the decisions that kept the work moving. The decision rights, the process ownership, and the performance metrics all assume that bottleneck. When you drop AI on top of that model, the AI inherits the same constraints. It accelerates handoffs that still feed the same approver. It generates outputs that still require the same review. It produces drafts that still die in the same inbox.

This is where the people work begins, and where most leadership teams stop. AI value does not come from automating what the team already does. It comes from redesigning how work flows through the organization. That redesign requires three things to move together: decision rights, process ownership, and the metrics that govern what good looks like. None of those live with the CTO. They live with the leadership team. And the leadership team has to say so, repeatedly. They have to use concrete examples from inside the business. Until the rest of the organization stops hearing AI project and starts hearing we are changing how the work runs.

The Operating Model OS test

For one workflow where AI has been deployed, ask three questions. Who owns the decision now that the AI surfaces it faster? Who owns the process now that the steps have changed? What metric is the work measured by now that throughput is no longer the constraint? If the answers are the same as they were before the AI showed up, the AI is decorative. You have not changed the operating model. You have made yesterday faster.

This week

Pick the one AI deployment your team talks about most. Sit with your leadership team for thirty minutes. Walk through the three questions above for that single workflow. Whatever does not have a clean answer is the work.

THE FIELD REPORT

KeyBank's Middle Market Snapshot survey finds that mid-market borrowers are increasingly using private capital as a growth lever, not a rescue mechanism. According to Middle Market Growth, CFOs and owners are playing offense - structuring capital to fund strategic moves rather than bridging distress Source.

Our view is that the structural lesson is about sequencing, not financing. The owners doing this well are not simply accessing cheaper capital. They are arriving at lender conversations with a defined use case - an acquisition, a capacity expansion, a market entry - and building the capital stack around it. That is a different posture than raising money and then deciding what to do with it.

Most mid-market operators still approach private capital reactively. The survey suggests the competitive gap is opening between those who treat capital as a strategic input and those who treat it as a last resort.

For the $5M-$100M owner, the implication is preparation. The time to map your capital options is before you need them, when your leverage is highest and your choices are widest.

Watch for this week: Count the decisions that escalated to you out of an AI-enabled workflow. Not how often the tool was used. Not how many prompts were run.
