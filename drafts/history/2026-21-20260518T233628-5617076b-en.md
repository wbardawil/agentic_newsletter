## THE APERTURA

The companies most likely to fail in integration are the ones that looked cleanest in the data room. The pattern surfaces again this week in [ACG's coverage of why M&A deals disappoint](https://middlemarketgrowth.org/cla-m-a-deals-fail-diligence-dilemma/). Deals appearing compelling on paper, CLA notes, frequently underperform expectations, destroy shareholder value, or unravel during integration. The reason is structural, not analytical. A clean data room means the founder is good at documenting. It does not mean the business can operate without him. Diligence captures the artifacts. It misses the dependency. By the time anyone names the dependency, the deal already closed at the price that assumed it did not exist.

## THE INSIGHT

Most advisors call this an M&A diligence problem and prescribe more diligence. It is not a diligence problem. It is an operating model problem that diligence cannot see.

The pattern plays out the same way every time. The deal closes. The numbers checked out. The integration team arrives Monday morning, ready to run the playbook. Within thirty days, decisions start stalling. Vendor renewals sit. Pricing exceptions wait. A customer escalation surfaces and nobody knows who decides. The seller's team looks at the buyer's team. The buyer's team looks at the seller's team. Both look for the founder.

This is the diagnosis the data room cannot deliver. The seller's leadership team has never operated without the founder's real-time input. Every meaningful decision routed through one person for years. That is how the business grew. The financials capture the result. They do not capture the dependency. By the time the integration team feels the dependency, the 90-day window is half over and the value case is leaking.

**The pre-close coalition.**

The Operating Model OS has a discipline most M&A processes skip entirely: ratifying the decision-rights structure before the deal closes, not during integration. The buyer and seller name a small group of leaders (usually three to five people drawn from both sides) who will hold authority over the operating model during the transition. They meet before close. They agree on which decisions each member owns. They publish the structure to both organizations.

This is not a committee. It is the artifact that lets the business run when the founder steps back. The buyer's integration team gains context. The seller's leadership team gains authority. The founder gains a defined role with a clear exit ramp. The 90-day window stops being a trust crisis and becomes a structured handoff.

The shift this requires is uncomfortable for the founder. For years the team learned to wait for his input. And he learned to give it. The coalition asks both sides to break that pattern on a date certain. Some founders cannot do it. The ones who can are the ones whose deals close at the price the model said they would.

**This week.**

If you are preparing to sell, write down the five decisions you made last week that nobody on your team was authorized to make on their own. That list is your integration risk register. Hand it to your advisor before the next diligence call. If you are preparing to buy, ask for the seller's version of that list. Whether you get it tells you what you are actually buying.
