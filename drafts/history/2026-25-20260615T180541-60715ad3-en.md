## THE INSIGHT

You have hired well. The team is competent. They still produce decisions that are not quite right, so you step in. You correct the work, redo the conversation, send the email yourself. Each correction confirms what your team already suspected. The right answer lives in your head, and the safest move is to wait for it. You built a business that runs on your judgment, and it now cannot run without it.

The misdiagnosis is that your team is not good enough. The real diagnosis is that you never wrote down the criteria you use. They reproduce every recurring judgment by reading your face. They get close. Eighty percent close. You hold the work to ninety-five, and the gap between eighty and ninety-five is the gap they cannot cross without you. So they stop trying.

The hard part is not the rule. The hard part is accepting that eighty percent is enough. Most owners cannot. They built the business on being the best operator in the room, and being the best is the identity they cannot put down. The shift the work asks of you is not delegation. It is letting your business run at eighty percent quality. Ten times more often than you could run it at ninety-five.

Operating Model OS has one artifact that does this work. We call it the Decision Rule, and it has three components.

The trigger names the recurring situation in language anyone in the seat would recognize. A vendor invoice over a threshold. A discount request from an account in a specific tier. A hire below the director level. The trigger is not the decision. It is the moment the decision begins.

The test names the criteria. Not the answer. The criteria the answer must meet. Two or three conditions, written in language a new hire could apply. The discipline is that the test contains your judgment, not your conclusion. You are not deciding for them. You are giving them the rule you used.

The boundary names what falls outside the rule and still belongs to you. Above this number. Above this seniority. Outside this customer segment. The boundary tells your team exactly when to bring the decision back. Without a boundary, they bring everything.

This week, pick one decision that came to you three or more times this quarter and should not have. Write its three components on one page. Trigger, test, boundary. Hand the page to the person whose seat the decision belongs in. Then do the harder thing. The next time the decision comes back to you, send it back. Not with the answer. With the page.

## THE FIELD REPORT

When Sumit Singh joined Chewy as COO in 2017, customer care reported to him. He has [told Fast Company](https://www.fastcompany.com/91557196/chewys-ceo-is-chasing-empathy-at-scale) he initially thought of it as a cost center. He says that was the wrong way to think about it in a category where empathy matters. He became CEO the following year.

The reframe became operational. Chewy did not staff up customer care as a margin drag to be optimized down. It built the function as the channel through which customer loyalty compounds. [Net sales topped $2.1 billion the year Singh joined](https://www.fastcompany.com/91557196/chewys-ceo-is-chasing-empathy-at-scale), more than double the prior year. Chewy is now the third-largest direct-to-consumer e-commerce network in the US.

For US mid-market owners, the lesson is structural. The cost-center label is rarely a financial classification. It is a decision rule the owner inherited and never rewrote. The categories you treat as cost determine which capabilities you let compound, and which ones quietly cap the business.

## THE TOOL

**The Decision Rule One-Pager** - A single page with three fields: trigger (when does this decision come up), test (two or three criteria the right answer must meet), boundary (when does it leave the rule and come back to you). Most $5M-$100M operators have no place to put their judgment outside their own head, so it never leaves. Build it in [Notion](https://www.notion.so) or a Google Doc. Tag it with the role that owns it, not the person.
