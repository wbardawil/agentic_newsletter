THE APERTURA

Most AI pilots in mid-market companies are not failing because the technology is weak. They are failing because they are being deployed into processes no one has documented, owned, or designed for handoffs between functions. Small business AI spend now runs at a median of $21 per employee, nearly double the cross-industry median, according to Business Insider. Some owners report accidentally running up bills they did not expect. The tool is not the problem. The problem is what happens when a decision engine lands inside an operating model that has never been written down.

THE INSIGHT

You bought the AI tool. Your team used it for a month, maybe two. The pilot slowed. The workaround returned. The invoice kept arriving. Six months later, you are quietly wondering whether the technology was oversold, whether your team is behind, or whether you moved too early.

None of that is the diagnosis.

The tool did not fail because it was bad technology. It failed because it was deployed into a process no one had ever documented. No owner. No written handoff between the person who starts the work and the person who finishes it. No agreement on what "done" means. The tool did exactly what it was designed to do. It accelerated whatever workflow it landed in. And the workflow it landed in was invisible.

This is the reframe most operators miss. AI does not fix an operating model. AI amplifies one. If the process is documented, owned, and designed for handoffs, the tool compresses time. If the process lives in three people's heads, the tool multiplies the ambiguity. Then it hides that ambiguity behind a cleaner-looking output.

Operating Model OS begins with evidence the owner must confront personally. Not a consultant's finding. The owner's own data. Where did AI adoption stall inside the company? Which teams reverted to the manual workaround, and how quickly? Which handoffs still route through the owner's inbox? That evidence is the awareness the owner has to sit with before the team will have any desire to redesign the process. Without it, every redesign conversation feels theoretical. Every push to document feels like bureaucracy imposed from above.

In most mid-market firms, the tool arrives before the process map. The vendor demo is impressive. The pilot is greenlit. The rollout begins. Nowhere in that sequence does anyone write down how the work actually moves. Marketing to operations. Sales to delivery. The person who takes the call to the person who fulfills it. What typically happens next is predictable. The tool sits on top of a process no one owns. The failure gets blamed on adoption.

The action this week is small and uncomfortable. Pick the one AI tool your team stopped using or is quietly working around. Pull the data on when adoption fell off. Then map the underlying process on a single page: trigger, owner, handoff, decision, close. If any of those five slots is empty, you have found where the tool was going to fail before it was installed. Fix the process on paper first. The tool comes after.
