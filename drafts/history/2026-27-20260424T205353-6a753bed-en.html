Option A - provocation

Every time a senior person leaves your company, they are telling you something about your operating model. You usually hear it as feedback on themselves. It is feedback on you. The best ones leave quietly. They say the right things on the way out. They do not tell you that the job they accepted and the job they found were different jobs. I have watched this pattern in three different companies this quarter. The owner believes he has a retention problem. He has an architecture problem. The people he hired to run parts of the business discovered there were no parts to run.

THE INSIGHT

You lost another senior leader this quarter. You paid market. You gave the title. You thought you gave real autonomy. They left anyway, and the reason on the way out was something vague about the next chapter. You tell yourself it is the market.

It is not the market. It is your operating model.

Here is what most advisors miss. Senior people do not leave well-run companies for more money. They leave companies where the work they were hired to do does not actually exist. You hired a VP of Operations to run operations. Then every operational decision above a small threshold still routes to you. The VP you hired is not running operations. She is translating operations to you for approval. After eighteen months of that, she finds a job where the title matches the work.

The pattern is invisible to the owner because you experience your involvement as care. You are close to the business. You are accessible. You weigh in because you have context nobody else has. All of that is true. It is also the ceiling your senior hires keep hitting.

The tenure test

There is a test for whether your operating model exists or whether you are it. I call it the tenure test. I have not seen it named this way in the retention literature. It is not a survey. It is not a values exercise. It is a question you can answer about the last three senior people who left.

For each of them, name the largest decision they owned end-to-end without your input. Not 'with your input and then they executed.' End-to-end. A hire, a termination, a vendor change, a policy rewrite, a pricing move, a product kill. Something real.

If you cannot name one decision for one of them, you have your answer. The operating model you believe exists does not exist at the level you hired them to run. They did not leave you. They left the gap between the job posting and the job.

This is the diagnostic underneath every retention conversation in a ten-to-fifty-million-dollar company. The owner believes senior tenure is a people problem. It is an architecture problem. The people are the symptom.

This week

Write down the last three senior leaders who left. For each, write the single largest decision they owned end-to-end without you. If you cannot name one, you have identified the exact layer of your operating model that does not yet exist. That is where the work is. Not in the next hire.

THE FIELD REPORT

Fast Company reported this week that every cofounder of xAI other than Elon Musk has now exited the company, alongside dozens of engineering and program staff. The exits overlap with a significant share of the leaders meant to direct the startup under a new organizational structure announced only in February.

They arrive as the company pursues a merger with SpaceX, a $60 billion option to acquire Cursor, and an upcoming public offering.

The US operator reading this at $20 million in revenue is tempted to dismiss the story as founder-celebrity noise. It is the same pattern at a larger scale. When the operating model is the founder's presence, the senior people you hire to run it discover there is nothing to run. They leave. You hire replacements who discover the same thing.

The question is not whether you will ever run a $60 billion company. It is whether the senior leaders you already have would describe their role the way your org chart does.

THE TOOL

Decision Ownership Log - a one-page document where each senior leader lists the five largest decisions they owned end-to-end last quarter, with no owner input on the final call. It reveals in minutes whether your operating model has layers or whether you are the only layer. Build it in Notion or a Google Doc - the format matters less than the question it forces.
