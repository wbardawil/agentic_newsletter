THE APERTURA

Succession is the only operating problem owners try to solve by hiring themselves a replacement. The Chief Executive piece this week is written by a founder who inherited his family company at 29 and, fifteen years in, says letting go is harder than taking hold. He had the title. He had the building. He still describes the work of release as the harder problem. That is the part nobody warns the next generation about. The successor is not waiting for the office. They are waiting for the decisions the office was supposed to come with.

THE INSIGHT

You named a successor. You gave them the title. You may have written it into the org chart. And the decisions that matter still land on your desk.

You watch them defer to you on calls. You watch them check in before they commit. You tell them, again, to make the call. They nod. The next decision still routes through you.

The conventional reading is that they are not ready. The honest reading is that you have not transferred what you said you transferred.

You handed over responsibility. You kept authority. Those are not the same thing.

Responsibility is the work of running a function. Authority is the right to decide without your sign-off. Most owners hand over the first and hold the second without naming it, because the second is what built the business. The successor inherits a job, not an ownership stake in the decision. They learn fast that the company is still yours. They start to manage you instead of the business.

This is the part the conventional advisor misses. They prescribe more time, more coaching, more clarity in the org chart. The org chart is not the bottleneck. The decision rights are.

There is a second cost, less visible. The longer the successor decides under your shadow, the smaller the ownership opportunity they think they are getting. In time they leave, or they stop pushing, or they accept the title without the role. The transfer fails before it starts.

Inside Operating Model OS, the authority transfer has three layers, and they have to move in sequence. Scope: name the categories of decisions that are theirs alone, like hiring below a level, pricing inside a band, vendor contracts under a threshold. Threshold: the dollar amount or risk boundary above which you want to be consulted, not asked. Reversal rights: who can override the successor's decision, when, and through what process.

Most owners hand over scope and silently keep reversal. The team learns this within a quarter. They route around the successor to you, and the title becomes decoration. The order is non-negotiable: scope, threshold, reversal. Once reversal is written down, you have to use it sparingly enough that it remains a backstop, not a default.

The work is yours to do, and it is harder than naming the successor. You are being asked to trust an incomplete leader with an incomplete system. That is the opposite of how you built the company. That trust is the actual transfer. Everything else is paperwork.

This week, pull the last ten decisions you made. For each one, ask: should this have been my successor's? Then write the threshold above which you still want to be consulted. The gap between what you decided and what you would now delegate is your real succession map. Show it to your successor. The conversation it starts is the one you have been postponing.

The strategic read is that Yum could not get the returns it wanted from Pizza Hut inside the portfolio. The operating model read is more interesting. LongRange is buying a brand the parent could not, or would not, run the way private capital believes it should be run. The thesis is operational, not strategic.

The pattern shows up at the lower end of the market too. Trulink Capital announced an investment this week in Horwitz, a mechanical, electrical, and plumbing services provider, per the same Middle Market Growth brief. The capital is following operating models that an owner-operator built but never finished documenting.

For a US owner of a $5M to $100M business, the buyer's first ninety-day question is not what your strategy is. It is who decides what, and where it is written down. If the answer is the owner, in their head, the multiple compresses before due diligence closes.

THE TOOL

Decision Rights One-Pager (built with Claude or ChatGPT) - A single page that lists the twenty most common recurring decisions in your business and assigns each to a role with a dollar or risk threshold. Why it matters for a $5M to $100M owner: it forces the conversation you have been avoiding and gives your successor something to point to when the team tries to route around them. Start by pasting the last thirty days of decisions you made into the model and asking it to cluster them, then propose owner, threshold, and reversal rights for each cluster.

THE COMPASS

Watch for this week: the decisions that land in your inbox that your named, or unnamed, successor should be making. Count them. The count is the diagnostic, not the content. If the number is above five, your transfer is not underway. If it is below five, notice which specific ones still came to you and why. That distinction is where the real succession work lives.
