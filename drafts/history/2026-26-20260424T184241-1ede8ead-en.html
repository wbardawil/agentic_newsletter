Option A - observation

An owner called me yesterday. He had signed the contract three months ago. The agent platform was live, technically. His team was still doing the work the old way. He asked me what went wrong with the vendor. Nothing went wrong with the vendor. The process the tool was meant to automate had never been written down. It lived in two people's heads, one of whom had left in February. The platform was working as designed. It had nothing to work on. The invoice kept arriving anyway, and the owner kept asking the wrong question.

THE INSIGHT

You bought the tool. Your team keeps doing the work the old way. You did not fail at change management. You failed at sequence.

Most owners read tech regret as a training problem. Their people resisted the new system. The rollout was rushed. The vendor oversold. So the fix is another round of training, a stricter mandate, a new vendor next quarter. None of it sticks, because the diagnosis is wrong.

Software does not automate work. It automates an encoded version of work. When the process lives in two senior people's heads, the tool has nothing to automate. What you bought is not a productivity system. You bought a very expensive storage container waiting for a document you never wrote.

A fresh Accenture and Wharton study, reported by HR Executive, shows that nearly three-quarters of US small and mid-sized businesses are re-evaluating their current tech stack. The narrative is buyer's remorse. The reality is something different. These companies did not choose the wrong tool. They installed the right tool on a floor that could not hold it.

The three conditions

Before any tool can compound value inside the Operating Model OS, three things must exist underneath it. The process must be written, in enough detail that a new manager could run it without asking. The decision rights must be named, so the tool knows which choices to surface and which to execute. And the data must live in one place the tool can read, not scattered across spreadsheets, inboxes, and a senior person's memory.

I have yet to meet a $5M to $100M owner who has all three in place before the tool arrives. Most have zero. They buy the platform first, because the platform is visible and the floor underneath it is not. The invoice comes in dollars. The absence comes in silence. The tool sits unused, and the owner blames the team.

This is the layer most companies never diagnose. You cannot skip what lives below the tool. You can delay it, but you pay for the delay every time a vendor renews and nothing has changed.

This week

Pick the tool in your stack that has disappointed you most. Do not cancel it. Do not call the vendor. Instead, write down the process it was supposed to automate, on one page, in full. If you cannot write it, you have named your real problem, and it is not the software. If you can write it, compare what you wrote to what the tool is actually doing. The gap tells you exactly what to fix, and it was never a feature.
