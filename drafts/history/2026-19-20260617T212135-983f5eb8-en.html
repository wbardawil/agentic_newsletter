THE APERTURA

The most discussed dysfunction in this week's coverage is the one founders are least willing to name. Chief Executive Magazine, Family Business Magazine, and an American Textile Company CEO interview each circle the same diagnosis from different angles. The board you assembled to challenge you has quietly become the board you assembled to agree with you. The shift was not a betrayal. It was a slow, mutual accommodation. You stopped bringing real questions. They stopped offering real answers. Both sides preferred the comfort, and neither side noticed the cost until a decision went sideways and no one in the room had questioned it.

THE INSIGHT

Your board has stopped surprising you. The meetings still happen. The agenda still circulates. The minutes still get filed. But sometime in the last eighteen months, you noticed something. The recommendations you bring in are the recommendations that walk out. Slightly polished. Never overturned.

Most advisors call this an alignment problem and prescribe better board composition. It is not an alignment problem. The board you built is doing exactly what you trained it to do.

You recruited people who share your values. That part you got right. Then you confused shared values with shared judgment. You began bringing the board decisions you had already made, looking for validation dressed as deliberation. The board learned the game. They became the room where you rehearse, not the room where you get challenged.

Strategy OS treats the board as one specific instrument: a counterweight to founder certainty. The shared values are the floor. They make the room safe enough to disagree. They are not the conclusion. They are the precondition for genuine disagreement.

A working board runs two registers. Values alignment asks one question: do we agree on what this company is for, and on who we will not become. Judgment alignment asks a different one. Do we agree this acquisition is the right move. Do we agree this hire is the right person. Do we agree this pricing change is the right risk. The first register must be shared. The second must be contested.

When the two registers collapse into one, the founder hears agreement on judgment as proof of values. Disagreement on judgment becomes evidence of misalignment. That is the trap. It is also the moment the board stops being useful.

What this asks of you is harder than a new board charter. It asks you to surrender the psychological safety of being right in every room. The board you actually need is the one that can tell you no without you flinching. You build that board by inviting the no, not by tolerating it. The first time the room pushes back and you do not defend, the room learns it is allowed to push again.

This week, pick one decision you are eighty percent convinced about. Walk it into the next board meeting as a question, not a recommendation. Present three options. Say which one you prefer and why. Then stop talking and watch what happens.

If the room moves to ratify your preference, you built a validator board. If someone challenges the frame itself, not the option but the question, you built a counterweight board. Most founders find out, in that single meeting, which board they actually built.

THE FIELD REPORT

In a 48-hour window this month, a single executive's conviction removed a tool from every operator who had built around it. Amazon CEO Andy Jassy raised concerns with Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent about researchers extracting cyberattack-relevant information from Anthropic's Claude Fable 5, according to HR Executive reporting on Wall Street Journal and CNBC coverage.

White House officials convened. Security researchers tested Amazon's claims. President Trump signed a global freeze on foreign access to Fable 5 and Mythos 5. Anthropic shut off worldwide access to both models. Many of the company's own foreign-born researchers were blocked from the models they had been hired to build.

Read the timeline for what it reveals about vendor risk. The shutdown did not begin in a contract clause. It began in a conversation between two executives.

For a US-based owner running a $5M to $100M business, the operational implication is direct. If a vendor's continuity depends on one administration's confidence in one CEO's framing of one rival's product, that vendor is not a system. It is a position. Treat it accordingly in your continuity plan.

THE TOOL

The Two-Register Board Audit - A one-page review run before every board meeting. Column one names the values question on the table and whether the room is aligned. Column two names the judgment question on the table and where you genuinely disagree. If column two stays empty across three consecutive meetings, the board has shifted from counterweight to chorus. Build it in Google Docs, share it with the chair before the agenda goes out.

THE COMPASS

Watch for this week: how many decisions you walk into your board meeting carrying one recommended option versus three. Count the meetings, not the decisions. If you find yourself presenting a single preferred path more than once, you are using the board as ratifier. The number is a diagnostic on you, not on them. The counterweight you need begins with the question you bring.
