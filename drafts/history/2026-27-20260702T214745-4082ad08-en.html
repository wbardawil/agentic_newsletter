You built your business by holding every cross-border decision. That decision volume is now choking growth.

The pattern is familiar. An operator in Monterrey waits three days for a vendor approval. A team lead in Bogotá pauses a hire while the founder's inbox fills. This is not laziness or timidity. This is the operating model built when the company was smaller than it is today.

The instinct is to hire another executive or install a workflow tool. Neither solves the underlying problem. Jurisdictional Decision Architecture

The Operating Model OS treats cross-border authority as a mapping problem, not a delegation problem. You are not delegating. You are drawing jurisdictional lines.

Three layers. Layer one: decisions that stay with you regardless of geography, such as capital allocation above a threshold, strategic pivots, and senior hires in any country. Layer two: decisions the local operator makes and reports on, such as vendor selection within a budget, hiring below a level, and customer terms within a range. Layer three: decisions the local operator makes without reporting, such as scheduling, local supplier issues, and small operational calls.

The hardest work is layer two. Layer one is easy to name. Layer three is easy to release. In our experience, layer two is where founders hoard without realizing it. Letting go here requires trust. Trust that a local operator, in a different culture and regulatory context, can make a call the founder would have made differently. That trust does not appear from a speech. It appears from a written boundary, and the discipline of holding to it when a decision comes back different than expected.

There is a stage in every change process where the leadership work is not to inspire but to remove barriers. This is that stage. The barrier here is not policy. The barrier is the founder's own approval habit.
