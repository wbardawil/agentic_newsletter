## THE APERTURA

The most expensive person on your next deal is the controller who knows where everything is. [Middle Market Growth](https://middlemarketgrowth.org/qoeagent-ai-qoe-data-problem/) reports that quality of earnings teams now spend eight to fifteen hours per engagement just preparing the data: mapping the chart of accounts, reconciling bank statements, and decoding what the seller's controller named in his own private vocabulary. That work produces no analytical insight. It produces a translation. Every hour the buyer's team spends translating is an hour the seller's price is being quietly marked down. The same person who made the books work is also the person making them harder to value.

## THE INSIGHT

Your books make sense because your controller makes them make sense. That is not an operating model. That is a person, doing translation work in real time, in a chair that has not been redesigned since the company was a third of its current size.

You would not call this a problem. Most owners do not. The financial close happens. The reports get produced. The board materials get assembled. Cash is reconciled. Everything works. The dysfunction is invisible until something forces the books to be read by someone who has never spoken to your controller.

That is what the [Middle Market Growth](https://middlemarketgrowth.org/qoeagent-ai-qoe-data-problem/) story is actually reporting. Eight to fifteen hours per quality of earnings engagement, spent before any analysis begins, decoding what the seller's controller built in his own private vocabulary. The number is not a bookkeeping failure. It is the cost of legibility that was never designed into the system. The controller did the job he was hired to do. He made the books work for him. Nobody ever told him the books also had to work for a stranger.

This is where most advisors get the diagnosis wrong. They look at the chart of accounts and recommend a clean-up project. The clean-up project is the symptom of the work, not the work. The real work sits one level up. The controller has built her value on knowing where everything is. The Operating Model OS requires her to rebuild her value on architecting systems that make the business legible without her interpretation. Those are two different jobs. They use different time. They produce different evidence of progress. A controller who spends Tuesday translating looks productive. A controller who spends Tuesday redesigning the chart of accounts so it does not need translation looks slow.

The owner who confuses the two will keep the wrong one.

The shift is a skills shift, not a tools shift. New software on top of an illegible system gives you a faster illegible system. The skill the company needs from the finance seat is no longer interpretation. It is architecture. Naming conventions that an outsider can read. A close process that produces the same numbers regardless of who runs it. Documentation that survives the controller's vacation. This is the version of the role most controllers were never trained for and most owners have never asked for. The conversation has to be explicit, because the controller cannot do both jobs at once.

**This week.** Run the stranger test. Take your last twelve months of P&L and hand it to a peer CFO or a new advisor who has never seen your books. Ask them to tell you, in thirty minutes, what your top five expense categories actually contain, without asking you a question. The gaps in their answer are the gaps in your architecture. That list is the first draft of the conversation you have been postponing with the person who built the books.
