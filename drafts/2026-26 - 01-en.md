## THE INSIGHT

You keep buying software that does not get used. Your team nodded in the demos. They agreed in the kickoff. Six months in, the platform is logged into by two people, and one of them is you.

The instinct is to blame adoption. Call it a change management problem. Bring in a consultant to run trainings. None of it works, because adoption is not the problem.

The real diagnosis sits one layer up. The software was sold to you as a way to automate a process. But the process it was meant to automate was never written down. It lived in your head, in your operations director's head, in the senior account manager's muscle memory. Three versions of the same workflow, each one slightly different, none on paper.

The vendor did not know this. The vendor sold you a tool that encodes a process. You had no process to encode, only a set of habits that looked like one from the outside. When the software asked for inputs (the approval threshold, the escalation rule, the exception criteria) nobody in the building could answer, because nobody had ever written those rules down.

This is the pattern I see most often in Operating Model OS failures. Owners try to skip the artifact. They want the system without the document the system is supposed to run. They conflate "we do this every day" with "this is written down." They are not the same thing.

**The Pre-Automation Brief**

Before any operating model can be automated, it must exist as a document three people could read separately and execute the same way. I call this the pre-automation brief, because I do not see the concept named cleanly in the literature. It has three components. The inputs the process requires. The decisions the process makes, with the rule behind each decision. The outputs the process produces, with the standard for each one.

If you cannot write those three components in prose, on one page, before buying the tool, the tool will not stick. It has nowhere to run. The people executing the process will keep executing it from memory, because memory is the only version that actually exists.

**This week**

Pick the last piece of software your team is not using. Open a blank document. Write down, in prose, what the process the software was supposed to automate is actually supposed to do. If you cannot write it without calling three people to ask, you have found the gap. It is not an adoption problem. It is a missing artifact.

For a US mid-market owner, the read is direct. The operating model must be articulated before the tool is evaluated, not after. Buyers entering 2026 without that discipline will be the ones re-evaluating again in 2027.
