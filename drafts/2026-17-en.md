## THE INSIGHT

You bought the AI tool. Your team is using it in pockets. The productivity gain you were sold has not arrived. The reports still come in late. The approvals still bottleneck through the same people. The dashboard that was supposed to give you visibility is being updated by someone who copies data from another system into it. You assume the rollout was incomplete. You schedule another training session.

It is not a rollout problem. It is an operating model problem. And the people who built the operating model you have today are the ones quietly making sure the new tool does not change it.

Here is the mechanism. Most processes in a middle-market company were not designed. They accumulated. A manager added a step because a mistake happened once. Another manager added an approval because she wanted visibility. Over time, the process became a record of which managers wanted to be in which loops. When you deploy AI into that workflow, the AI handles the work fine. What it cannot handle is the politics. The step where Marcus has to review the file is still there. Not because Marcus adds judgment. Because Marcus's role is partly defined by being the person who reviews the file.

This is the diagnosis most owners miss. The managers blocking AI adoption are not blocking it because they do not understand the tool. They are blocking it because they understand exactly what it threatens. The process is the moat around their authority. Automate the process and the moat is gone.

The Operating Model OS asks a harder question than the productivity question. It asks three things. Which decisions in this workflow require human judgment. Which decisions require human accountability. And which 'decisions' are actually control checkpoints that produce nothing. The first two stay. The third must be reassigned before the tool is deployed. Not after. Before.

This requires you to do something most owners avoid. You have to look at each manager whose role includes approval authority and ask honestly whether the approval adds value or absorbs it. The managers who add value will welcome the tool, because it frees them from work that was never theirs. The managers who absorb value will resist it, by raising risks, by insisting on edge cases, by quietly slowing the project. Their resistance is the diagnosis. You wanted a signal for where the bottleneck lives. They are it.

**This week.** Pick one workflow where AI was deployed and adoption stalled. List every approval step in that workflow. For each approval, ask what would happen if the step was removed. If the honest answer is nothing bad, the approval was never decision authority. It was process ownership held in place to protect a role. Reassign it to the person actually doing the work. Then redeploy the tool.
