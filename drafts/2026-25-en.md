## THE INSIGHT

You think succession is a people problem. Who replaces your head of ops. Who takes over if your sales director leaves. Who could step in if you took ninety days off. You treat it as recruiting. Find the right person, put them in the seat, and the function continues.

Most advisors will agree with this framing. They will help you build bench strength, run development plans, design leadership tracks. None of it holds when the person actually leaves. The replacement walks in and nothing works the way it worked two weeks ago. You conclude you picked the wrong person. You did not pick the wrong person. You picked a person to fill a role that was never a role.

What you are actually losing when someone leaves is not their skills. It is the undocumented decisions they made each week. The standards they applied in their head. The relationships they maintained without a system. The routines that existed because they existed. The next hire inherits a ghost, not a function.

**The person-proof test**

The Operating Model OS has a simple diagnostic for this, what I call the person-proof test. For any critical role, ask one question: if this person left Monday with no notice, what specifically would stop working by Friday? The list is your operating model debt. It is the work that never got converted from person to system.

The debt lives in three layers. First, the recurring decisions. What does this role decide each week, on what inputs, by what rule? Second, the standards. What does 'good' look like in this function, and who gets looped in when something exceeds normal? Third, the handoffs. Who feeds this role and who receives from it?

An operating model that passes the person-proof test does not need heroes. That is the point. Not because people stop mattering. They matter more, because you finally have a structure they can contribute to instead of one they have to carry. When people leave, the machine keeps running. When you hire, the new person steps into a defined role instead of a person-shaped hole.

**This week**

Pick one critical function in your business. Not the one you are most worried about. The one you rely on most without thinking about it. Write down the three recurring decisions it makes. Not what you want it to do. What it actually does on a typical Tuesday. If you cannot write that down in one sitting, you have just diagnosed your first operating model gap. That gap is not a crisis. It is the starting point.

## THE FIELD REPORT

Meta will lay off 8,000 people on May 20. That is 10% of its global workforce. The company is also cancelling 6,000 planned hires. According to [El País reporting on the internal note](https://elpais.com/economia/2026-04-23/meta-despedira-a-cerca-de-8000-personas-el-10-de-su-plantilla-global-por-el-impacto-de-sus-inversiones-en-ia.html), the cuts are framed as an efficiency effort to offset the company's AI investments.

The headline reads as a tech story. It is not. It is an operating model story. Meta is betting that its AI investments will absorb work that humans were doing. That bet only works if the work being absorbed was already documented. You cannot hand a function to a model if the function lived in the individual doing it.

For US-based owners watching this: the question is not whether you will face the same pressure. You will. The question is whether your operating model is written down clearly enough for AI to take parts of it, or for a smaller team to run it. Meta's layoffs are not the warning. Meta's bet is. They are betting their documentation is good enough. Are you betting yours is?
