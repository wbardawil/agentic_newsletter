## THE APERTURA

Only twenty-three percent of senior leaders say their workforce is fully ready for the AI they have already deployed, [according to a new Kyndryl study of 1,100 leaders across eight countries](https://hrexecutive.com/kyndryl-chro-3-strategies-to-become-an-ai-pacesetter/). Down six points in a single year. Nearly eighty percent say the pace of AI is outrunning their workforce, governance, and operating models. Only nine percent qualify as "Pacesetters." The reading everyone reaches for is a skills problem. Buy more training. Run more workshops. It is not a skills problem. The tools were installed on top of roles nobody rewrote. The team is not slow. The job description was never updated.

## THE INSIGHT

You bought the AI. The team is using it, sort of. But the work is not moving faster. Decisions still route through you. The people you deployed the tool to sound polite in meetings and unconvinced in private. You call this an adoption problem and schedule more training.

It is not an adoption problem.

The mistake is upstream of the tool. In our experience, leaders deploy AI assuming the existing role definitions will absorb it. They will not. The role that existed before the tool was written for a job the tool has now partially taken over. No one has told the person in the role what their new job actually is. They still see the same title, the same objectives, the same escalation path. The AI shows up like an unpaid intern nobody asked for, doing pieces of their work while the boundaries of ownership stay unchanged.

The passive resistance you are reading as technical friction is something else. It is the rational response of a professional who has not been told what they now own. The tool quietly redistributes their work. No one names the new role. That feels less like an upgrade and more like a demotion in slow motion. Their silence is not confusion. It is self-protection.

This is where the Operating Model OS work begins, before any training deck is opened. The artifact required is a Role Redesign Map. For every role touched by the AI deployment, three questions have to be answered on paper, by name, by the leader. What decisions does this role now own that it did not own before? What work that previously belonged to this role now belongs to the tool? What new judgment must the human exercise on top of what the tool produces?

Those three answers are the new job. Until they exist in writing, you have not deployed AI. You have installed software on top of a workflow the team is now silently negotiating in the dark. The vision speech about AI will not close the gap. Only the redesign will, because it is the redesign that shows the person what the daily work now looks like. Not the strategy behind it.

This week, pick one role where AI is already deployed. Sit down for thirty minutes and answer the three questions. Write it out. Then share it with the person in that role and ask what you got wrong. The conversation that follows is the actual deployment. What you did last quarter was the invoice.
