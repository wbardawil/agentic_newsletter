## THE APERTURA

The expense is what the solo decision encodes. Every hire reflects criteria that live only in the owner's head. The team cannot replicate what it has never seen. The next twenty hires get narrower, not because the founder wanted that, but because the filter never got written down and never got tested against anyone else's read. ## THE INSIGHT

You hire by gut. It worked. Every key person on your team was your call, and most of them turned out to be the right call. The business exists because of that judgment.

Now the team is twenty, fifty, two hundred. You are still the only person who can decide whether someone is the right fit. The pattern that built the business has become the constraint on it.

Most owners diagnose this as a recruiting problem. They hire an HR lead. They buy an ATS. The decisions still route to the owner. The new system processes more candidates faster, but the final filter never moves.

The real diagnosis sits in two places. First, the hiring criteria never got converted into an artifact. They live in your head at full resolution and nowhere else. Your team cannot apply what they cannot see. Second, and this is the harder one, the instinct that built the company encodes biases you have not examined. People who feel right tend to look like the people who worked before. Your team gets narrower. Capability concentrates. The diversity of judgment your next phase requires does not arrive.

The Operating Model OS frames this as a missing Decision Rule for hiring. Three components. Trigger: the role and the moment the rule applies. Test: the specific criteria (skills, behaviors, evidence) that determine fit. Boundary: what makes the answer no regardless of how the conversation feels.

Writing the rule does two things at once. It gives your team something to apply without you. And it shows you, for the first time, what you have been filtering for. Some criteria will be about performance and should stay. Some will be about familiarity, comfort, or shared background. Those you confront.

The hardest part is not writing the rule. It is wanting someone else to use it. Wanting to be relieved of the hiring filter is the precondition. Without it, you will write the rule and override it the moment a candidate sits across from you. Agreeing in principle is not the same as wanting it.

This week, write the Decision Rule for the next role you are hiring. Trigger, test, boundary. Hand it to one person on your team and let them screen the first round without you. Read their notes before you meet anyone. The gap between their assessment and yours is the data. The question that matters is not whose read is right. It is what your read filtered out that you never noticed.

## THE FIELD REPORT

LongRange Capital is acquiring Pizza Hut from Yum Brands for $1.5 billion, [according to Middle Market Growth's PE Weekly](https://middlemarketgrowth.org/pe-weekly-pizza-hut-take-private-tech-deals/), with Yum China Holdings buying the Mainland China business for $1.2 billion. The chain has lost ground for years inside a parent company that prioritized Taco Bell and KFC.

The transaction reads as a financing event. It is an operating model event. Yum ran Pizza Hut as one brand inside a portfolio where capital, talent, and decisions flowed through corporate. LongRange is betting that pulling the chain out lets operational moves happen at a speed the parent-company structure could not support.

This is the thesis underneath most mid-market PE take-privates right now. The product is not broken. The system around it stopped scaling, and the people inside it stopped having the authority to fix it.

For US owners in the $5M-$100M range, the implication is direct. The businesses that get bought and turned around are not the ones with bad products. They are the ones whose operating model became the founder's preferences, frozen in place, with no one else authorized to change them.
