THE INSIGHT

Your revenue is up. Your team is bigger. You added tools. And yet every week you feel the same thing you felt last year, only with more zeros attached. More decisions in your inbox. More approvals. More people waiting on you.

The symptom looks like a scale problem. It is not. It is a compounding problem.

Most owners think growth produces capacity. It does not. Growth produces volume. Something else produces capacity: the institutional floor underneath the machine.

According to Expansión, Mexico has posted records in investment, remittances, and tax collection while GDP has grown near 2% for a decade. Economists call this a stagnation trap. Activity rises. The floor underneath it (rule of law, property rights, enforcement) does not. So effort never compounds into productivity.

Your company has its own version. Revenue grows. Headcount grows. Tools multiply. The floor underneath (the decision rights, the data ownership, the SLAs between functions) does not. The business grows in size but not in capacity. And you feel it first, because every decision the floor should absorb routes to you instead.

Operating Model OS requires three institutional artifacts before growth compounds. First, decision rights: who decides what, at what threshold, with what consultation. Second, SLAs between functions: what operations owes sales, what finance owes operations, what the timeline is when a commitment lands. Third, data ownership: which role owns each number, which role can change it, which role is accountable when it is wrong.

Most mid-market companies have none of these written down. The owner carries all three in his head. When the company was ten people, that worked. At forty, it does not. At eighty, it becomes the ceiling he keeps hitting and cannot name.

You can see the pattern from outside. A company that keeps posting records while the owner keeps getting more exhausted. The market is pulling. The floor is not holding. That gap is not a motivation problem or a talent problem. It is a governance problem dressed up as a scale problem.

This week, pick one decision that came to you three times in the last thirty days. Not the biggest one. The most recurring one. Write the rule that makes that decision without you. One paragraph. Who decides. At what threshold. With what inputs. Circulate it to the two people affected. Watch what comes back.

That paragraph is the first brick of your institutional floor. You will need eighty of them. You start with one.

THE FIELD REPORT

Warner Bros Discovery shareholders approved the $111 billion merger with Paramount this week, per Expansión. The vote clears one of the last obstacles to a deal that consolidates two major studios and CNN under one owner, pending regulatory approval.

Netflix withdrew its competing offer in February. The deal is expected to close later this year.

For a US-based operator running $5M-$100M, the signal is not entertainment. It is the pattern. When your largest customer, your largest vendor, or your largest distribution channel consolidates, the commercial terms you negotiated under the old structure expire the day the merger closes. The contracts may survive. The relationship that made them work does not. The people who gave you the handshake get new titles, new incentives, and new KPIs that do not include you.

Pull your top ten counterparties this quarter. For each, ask one question. If they were acquired next quarter, would our terms renew under a new owner? The companies that survive consolidation cycles are the ones that mapped relationship dependency before the call came.
