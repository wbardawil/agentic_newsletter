## THE INSIGHT

You bought the AI tool. The pilot worked. The rollout stalled. Your team is using it for what they used to do manually, faster by a margin, but the productivity case in the deck has not arrived.

Most owners read this as an adoption problem and prescribe more training. It is not an adoption problem. Your team is being asked to do something they have never been asked to do before. They are being asked to describe how their own work actually happens. They were hired to execute defined tasks. Nobody asked them to surface the workflow. Now you are asking them to redesign it for a machine, and they do not have the vocabulary or the visibility.

The Marsh and Mercer 2026 data names this from the outside. 40% of HR and risk leaders cite AI spend that runs ahead of employee enablement. Brunswick Corporation's CHRO put it more plainly: getting tech investments to pay off requires changing how work actually works. That sentence is the entire Operating Model OS argument compressed.

Here is the framework. Before any AI rollout, the team needs to produce the working version of the process. The policy version is what the procedure manual says. The working version is what actually happens: the controller who knows customer X gets net-45 even though the policy says net-30, because of a handshake in 2019. Every business runs on the working version. Almost none of them have written it down. AI cannot be pointed at the right seam until somebody does.

The reason this gets skipped is structural. The employees who run the process best are often the worst at describing it, because the knowledge is tacit. They have to be coached into seeing their own work as something separable from themselves. That coaching is the Operating Model OS investment that has to precede the Technology OS investment. Skip it, and the AI rollout becomes a license bill the CFO will eventually ask about.

This week, pick one process. Invoicing, quote-to-order, vendor onboarding. Ask the person who runs it to write down what they actually do, step by step, in the next seven days. Not the policy. The lived version. You will get something incomplete on the first pass. The gaps will be the most important information you receive this quarter, because every gap is a place where the AI cannot yet help.

The shift you are asking for is not a skill shift. It is a point-of-view shift. You are asking the person who has been the verb to become the noun, to see the work as an object instead of an action. That is a capability change. It is the work that has to land before the technology can.
