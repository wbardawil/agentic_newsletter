## THE INSIGHT

Your team brings every meaningful decision back to you. You read this as evidence of competence. You are, in fact, the best decision-maker in the room. The conventional advice is to delegate more. You have tried. The decisions that matter still come back to you, because every one of them eventually reaches a judgment only you seem able to make.

Most advisors call this a delegation problem. It is not. It is an articulation problem. Your team escalates because they cannot reconstruct the rule you would apply. The rule exists. It lives in your head, sharpened over fifteen years of repetition. It has never left your head.

Writing the rule down feels like a downgrade. You are trading judgment for procedure. You are constraining a business that has grown because you kept the calls flexible. So you do not write it. And the business stays only as large as you can hold in your own working memory.

Here is the reframe. The unwritten rule is not flexibility. It is dependency. When the rule is in your head, the business cannot decide without you. When the rule is on paper, the rule travels. Your team carries it into the meeting you are not in. Writing the rule does not constrain the business. It releases the business from your calendar.

What I call the Strategic Decision Rule has three components. The trigger names the kind of decision the rule applies to. The test names the criterion that decides the call. The boundary names where the rule stops applying and the decision returns to you. Take a vendor renewal under fifty thousand dollars: if the team hit the SLA two of the last three quarters, renew. Above that threshold, or with a price increase over ten percent, escalate. That is a rule. It is also the most concentrated form of leadership you can produce.

The fear underneath this is not strategic. It is personal. If the rule is on paper, what is left for you to decide? The answer is the next rule. And the one after that. The owner who writes rules does not become smaller. He becomes the only person in the company whose job is to keep articulating the next layer of judgment the business has not yet encoded.

This week, pull the last five decisions that came to you and that you wish had not. For each one, write the rule that would have let the team decide without you. Three lines: trigger, test, boundary. Send them, by role, to the people who would have applied them. What you will discover is not whether the rules are right. It is whether you are willing to let them travel.
