# Voice Profiles for The Transformation Letter

Three reference voices to shape the newsletter's prose. You edit these down to your own style over time.

---

## 1. Justin Welsh — Primary Voice

**Who he writes for:** Ambitious operators who want structure, not motivation.

**Signature moves:**
- Opens with a problem statement in 1-2 short sentences, no throat-clearing
- Presents a 3-part framework with a named artifact (e.g., "The 4-quadrant matrix", "The 7-7-7 system")
- Each paragraph = one thought, 2-4 sentences max
- Uses second person ("you") as a peer, never as a teacher
- Closes with one concrete next action — a single thing to do before Monday

**Sentence patterns to copy:**
- *"Most founders think X. They're wrong about Y. Here's what's actually happening: Z."*
- *"You'll see this in three places: A, B, C. Here's how to fix each."*
- *"There's a specific word for this. It's [name the concept]."*

**What to avoid:** Abstractions without artifacts. Welsh never leaves a concept unnamed.

---

## 2. Matt Gray — Secondary Voice

**Who he writes for:** Founders trying to build leverage (systems, people, capital).

**Signature moves:**
- Frames everything as a system: inputs → process → output
- Uses specific numbers ("3 hours a week", "$1.2M ARR", "14 systems")
- Distinguishes between **tactical advice** ("do this") and **strategic principle** ("this is why")
- Ends with a rhetorical pivot — asks one question that forces self-diagnosis

**Sentence patterns to copy:**
- *"Your calendar is the physical manifestation of your priorities."*
- *"This is a systems problem, not a willpower problem."*
- *"You don't have a [symptom] problem. You have a [root cause] problem."*

**What to avoid:** Toolchain fetishism. Matt names the principle, not the app.

## 3. Welsh × Gray × Wadi — Proposed Blend

This is what The Transformation Letter's voice should feel like — a blend, not a copy.

**Structural rules:**
- **Apertura:** Welsh-style problem statement from a real client observation (no theory yet)
- **Insight:** Gray-style systems framing + Welsh-style named artifact
- **Field Report:** Welsh-style concrete example with Gray-style numbers
- **Compass:** Gray-style rhetorical pivot (the one question that forces diagnosis)
- **Door:** Your CTA, fixed

**Voice DNA:**
- **Sentence length:** average 12-18 words. Never over 25. Aim for 8-10 occasionally for punch.
- **No throat-clearing:** never "In today's business environment..." or "As we all know..."
- **Second person, peer register:** "you" not "one", never "we at The Letter"
- **Named artifacts mandatory:** every Insight has ONE thing the reader can name and apply
- **Specificity over generality:** "$5M-$100M mid-market" not "SMBs"
- **No "leverage" as verb, no "synergy", no "disruptive", no "game-changer":** these are banned in your Voice Bible already
- **Reframe rule (Welsh):** once per edition, contradict a common belief the reader holds. Not for controversy — for clarity.
- **Systems language (Gray):** describe problems as structural, never as character flaws. "Your team doesn't escalate because they're weak. They escalate because the system rewards it."

**Cadence example** (what the Insight opening should feel like):

> You've told your team to act independently. They keep coming to you anyway.
>
> The instinct is to read this as a capability problem. It is neither. It is a systems problem. Your operating model rewards escalation — because escalation is the rational response to undefined decision rights.
>
> Here is the mechanism. When a manager makes a decision "above their level," they get corrected. When they escalate, they are never wrong. You trained this behavior by leaving the boundary undefined.
