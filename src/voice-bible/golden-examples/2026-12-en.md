# The Transformation Letter — Edition 2026-12

**Subject:** Your team isn't waiting on permission. They're following your system.
**Preheader:** The autonomy problem is not a people problem.

---

## THE APERTURA

A client messaged me on a Sunday afternoon. His team had spent three days unable to decide whether to renew a vendor contract. The decision had been sitting in someone's inbox while the vendor followed up twice. He was traveling. Nothing moved. He forwarded me the chain and wrote: why do they keep doing this? It was not a question about the vendor. When I read through the emails, I understood what he was actually looking at. Nobody had told his team that this decision was theirs to make. They were waiting because waiting is the rational response when the system has not given you permission to decide.

---

## THE INSIGHT

You have told your team to act independently. They keep coming to you anyway. You have said the words — make the call, use your judgment, I trust you. And still, the decisions land on your desk.

The instinct is to read this as a capability problem or a confidence problem. It is neither. It is a systems problem. Your team is doing exactly what the operating model you built tells them to do.

Here is the mechanism. When decision rights are undefined — when nobody has explicitly stated which decisions belong to which role — the rational behavior is to escalate. Escalation is not timidity. It is self-preservation. A manager who makes a decision that turns out to be "above their level" gets corrected. A manager who escalates is never wrong. You trained this behavior by leaving the boundaries undefined.

Every time a decision comes to you that shouldn't, you have two choices: answer it, or send it back. Most owners answer it. Answering is faster. It resolves the immediate problem. It also reinforces the pattern. You just confirmed, again, that this type of decision belongs to you.

**Decision Rights Architecture**

The Operating Model OS requires one structural artifact before autonomy becomes possible: a decision rights map. Not an org chart — a document that specifies which decisions each role owns fully, which decisions require consultation, and which decisions belong exclusively to ownership.

Three tiers. Tier one: decisions that require the owner. These exist, and they should be named explicitly — capital allocation above a threshold, strategic pivots, people decisions at the senior level. Tier two: decisions managers make and report on. They decide; you are informed, not consulted. Tier three: decisions the team makes entirely. They act; you learn from outcomes, not inputs.

The discipline in this framework is not in defining tier one. It is in defining tier two and three — the decisions you are currently holding that do not belong to you. Most owners have never mapped this. They know what they want to decide. They have never written down what they want to stop deciding.

When you write it down, something changes. Your team stops asking — not because they found courage, but because they found clarity. The ambiguity that made escalation rational is gone.

**This week**

Pull the last ten decisions that came to you. For each one, ask a single question: should this have reached me? If the honest answer is no, write down whose decision it actually is. That list is the first draft of your decision rights map. Share it with your team, specifically, by role. The conversations it starts will tell you where the real ambiguity lives.

## THE FIELD REPORT

A Colombian logistics company operating across the Bogotá–Miami corridor recently restructured its management team — not by adding headcount, but by redistributing decision authority. The trigger was a six-week period in which three operational decisions stalled because the owner was managing a client crisis and unavailable for approvals.

The restructuring did not involve new hires or a new org chart. It involved a single document: a decision rights framework that assigned sixty-three recurring decisions to specific roles with defined approval thresholds. Decisions that had previously required owner sign-off — vendor selection below a cost threshold, hiring at the coordinator level, customer credit terms within a range — were explicitly reassigned.

Within ninety days, the owner reported a measurable reduction in the volume of decisions escalated to him each week.

For corridor operators managing businesses across two regulatory environments, distributed decision authority is not a luxury. It is the architecture that allows the business to function when the owner is in the other country.

## THE COMPASS

I have been sitting with this question lately: how much of what I call leadership is actually decision-hoarding — and would I know the difference? There is a version of staying close to decisions that is genuine stewardship. And there is a version that is control dressed as care. I am not sure I always know which one I am practicing in the moment. That uncertainty feels important to stay with rather than resolve too quickly.
